1 Introduction

This report presents the results of an analysis comparing the effectiveness of three different drugs in reducing systolic blood pressure (SBP). SBP is a critical measure of cardiovascular health, representing the force exerted by the blood against the artery walls when the heart beats 1. Elevated SBP is a known risk factor for serious conditions such as heart disease and stroke, making its reduction a key target in medical treatment.

An experiment was conducted to attempt to determine what an effective treatment is for high SBP. In this report, we will address the following:

  • Assess if the three drugs are equally effective in lowering mean SBP.
  • Identify the most effective drug
  • Evaluate the validity that the subject were indeed randomly assigned to drug treatments.

By conducting this analysis, we aim to provide clear insights into the comparative effectiveness of the drugs and ensure that the experiment was conducted in a fair and ethical way. These findings will help inform future decisions on the optimal treatment for reducing SBP.

2 Methods

2.1 Data

We analyze the data compiled by the client during their experiment. Twelve individuals had their initial SBP in millimeters of mercury (mmHg) measured and then four were randomly assigned to each of the three treatment groups. After administration of the respective drug for the duration of the experiment (which is unspecified) the SBPs of each subject were measured again.

The data contains only the four variables: the subject, their assigned treatment and their SBP before and after the experiment. No other information about subjects or how the experiment was conducted are available.

2.2 Analysis

In this analysis, we will primarily be using one-way Analysis of Variance (ANOVA) to assess the differences in mean response (absolute and relative reduction in SBP) given the drug the subject was treated with. The usual additive model is given in the form

\[ \begin{equation} \tag{1} Y_{ij} = \mu + \tau_j + \epsilon_{ij} \end{equation} \]

where

  • \(Y_{ij}\) is the response of the \(i^{th}\) subject from the \(j^{th}\) treatment group (\(Y_{ij} = |\delta_{ij}|\) or \(Y_{ij} = |\delta\%_{ij}|\)).

  • \(\mu\) is the overall mean response from all subjects.

  • \(\tau_j\) is the added (or differential) effect of the \(j^{th}\) treatment on \(\mu\).

  • \(\epsilon_{ij}\) is the error on the response for the \(i^{th}\) subject from the \(j^{th}\) treatment group.

Additionally, we have that

\[ \sum_{j=1}^m \tau_j = 0, \epsilon_{ij} \sim N(0, \sigma^2) \]

For convenience, we will work with the equivalent linear regression form of the ANOVA model:

\[ \begin{equation} \tag{2} Y_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1X_1 + \beta_2X_2 + \epsilon_{ij} \end{equation} \]

where

  • \(X_1 = \begin{cases} 1, Moexipril \\ 0, otherwise \end{cases}\) indicates if subject \(i\) belongs to the Moexipril group.

  • \(X_2 = \begin{cases} 1, Ramipril \\ 0, otherwise \end{cases}\) indicates if the subject \(i\) belongs to the Ramipril group.

  • \(\beta_0\) is the true mean response for people taking Lisinopril group.

  • \(\beta_1\) is the true mean difference in response of people taking Moexipril from those taking Lisinopril.

  • \(\beta_2\) is the true mean difference in response of people taking Ramipril from those taking Lisinopril.

The assumptions of the ANOVA model are as follows:

  • Independence The observations should be independent of each other.

  • Normality The distribution of the residuals (or responses) are normally distributed.

  • Homoscedasticity The variance within each treatment group is the same.

  • Randomization The experiment should follow a completely randomized design. Each subject was randomly assigned to a treatment group.

The assumption of independence is difficult to verify, but given the purported random assignment of subjects, there is little reason to suspect dependence between observations. The assumption of normality, as checked by a QQ-plot, does not hold due to the small sample size, but the ANOVA model is robust to this violation. Homoscedasticity is also hard to assess with so few observations, especially given a potential outlier in the Lisinopril group, but again, ANOVA can handle some deviation from this assumption. While a Box-Cox transformation improved homoscedasticity, the loss of interpretability may outweigh the benefits given the limited data.

2.3 Randomization

To assess whether the twelve subjects were randomly assigned to treatment groups, we should consider the distributional differences between the characteristics of the subject before the experiment began. The researchers have no way of knowing how the subjects will react to the drugs, so the ending blood pressure can be ignored. The only available prior information is the starting blood pressure. It is presumable that the researchers probably had other information about each subject before the experiment began, such as their age, weight, sex, etc. But in the provided data we only have their starting blood pressures to work with. If we had the other contextual information about each subject, then more analysis could be done.

We can also perform a test to see if the subjects were randomly assigned. A randomization test (or permutation test) is a non-parametric test used to test the null hypothesis that there is no difference between groups or treatments. It works by comparing an observed test statistic (such as the F-statistic) to a distribution of test statistics generated by randomly reassigning or “permuting” the group labels. The idea is to simulate what the data would look like under the null hypothesis, where any observed differences are due to chance alone. In each permutation, the group labels are shuffled, but the actual data (the starting blood pressures) stay the same. After repeated random permutations, the observed test statistic of the actual data and true labels is compared to the distribution of permuted test statistics with the swapped labels. The proportion of permuted test statistics that are as extreme as or more extreme than the observed one gives the p-value, which reflects the likelihood of observing such a result if the null hypothesis were true. The non-parametric nature of the test makes it robust to assumption violations and small sample sizes. For details on the insight and performance of a randomization test, see 2.

3 Results

3.1 EDA

One of the major questions we must ask ourselves when working with this data is “How do we measure effectiveness of a medication?” We could consider the absolute (raw) change in SBP, as in equation \((3)\) but this difference would not take into account those with already fairly lower SBPs. Indeed, two individuals could have starting SBPs of \(180\) mmHg and \(130\) mmHg. The former could see a reduction to \(172\) (a reduction of \(18\)) mmHg while the ladder could drop to \(117\) mmHg (a reduction of \(13\)). Under this metric, the former did better even though they still have a very high blood pressure. Both represent a \(10\%\) reduction in SBP during the treatment period, so relative or percentage change in SBP (see equation \((4)\)) could better account for the starting SBP before beginning treatment.

\[ \begin{equation} \tag{3} |\delta| = |Y_{before} - Y_{after}| \end{equation} \]

\[ \begin{equation} \tag{4} |\delta\%| = 100*\frac{|\delta|}{Y_{before}} = 100*|\frac{Y_{before} - Y_{after}}{Y_{before}}| \end{equation} \]

3.2 Models

With three treatment groups to compare, and when considering the mean absolute or percentage reduction in systolic blood pressure for each group, it is natural to want to continue with the analysis of variance (ANOVA). However, we also consider some alternative methods that could be more accurate than the one-way ANOVA. In this model, we fit the equation

\[ \begin{equation} \tag{5} |\delta|_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1X_1 + \beta_2X_2 + \epsilon_{ij} \end{equation} \]

or similarly,

\[ \begin{equation} \tag{6} |\delta\%|_{ij} = \beta_0 + \beta_1X_1 + \beta_2X_2 + \epsilon_{ij} \end{equation} \]

where \(|\delta|_{ij}\) is the absolute reduction in SBP for the \(i^{th}\) subject in the \(j^{th}\) treatment group and \(|\delta\%|_{ij}\) is the relative or percentage reduction in SBP given the starting blood pressure of the \(i^{th}\) subject in the \(j^{th}\) treatment group.

However, both \((5)\) and \((6)\) suffer from a similar shortcoming. They assume that the starting SBP has a fixed or constant effect on the post experiment SBP. To remedy this, we fit a slight variation of the model

\[ \begin{equation} \tag{7} Y_{after}^{(ij)} = \beta_0 + \beta_1X_1 + \beta_2X_2 + \beta_3Y_{before}^{(ij)} + \epsilon_{ij} \end{equation} \]

where

  • where \(Y_{after}^{(ij)}\) is the systolic blood pressure of the \(i^{th}\) subject in the \(j^{th}\) treatment group after having taken the \(j^{th}\) treatment for the period of the experiment.

  • \(Y_{before}^{(ij)}\) is the systolic blood pressure of the \(i^{th}\) subject in the \(j^{th}\) treatment group before the beginning of the experiment.

  • \(\beta_3\) is the true mean change in \(Y_{after}^{(ij)}\) given a unit increase in \(Y_{before}^{(ij)}\)

In this variation of the ANOVA model, we use the starting blood pressure as a covariate to the ending blood pressure. We are effectively saying that the starting blood pressure will effect someones ending blood pressure. This is called the Analysis of Covariance (ANCOVA) model. For details on all of these models, see 9.

4 Conclusion

4.1 Findings

Based on our analysis, Moexipril appears to be the most effective drug in reducing mean systolic blood pressure, with an estimated reduction of approximately 4.2 mmHg more than Lisinopril and over 16 mmHg more than Ramipril. Regarding the randomization of subjects, we find no evidence of foul play in the assignment of treatments. While we cannot definitively confirm the legitimacy of randomization without consulting the researchers, there is no reason to suspect any irregularities.

4.2 limitations

However, we strongly caution against drawing any firm conclusions or taking immediate action based on these results. The study’s small sample size of only 12 subjects, with just four replicates per treatment group, limits the reliability of the findings and makes the results particularly vulnerable to the influence of outliers and violation of model assumptions. Additionally, because we lack information about other subject characteristics beyond their systolic blood pressures, various external factors could be impacting the outcomes, further reducing the certainty of our conclusions. We are also ignorant to the specifics of how the experiment was conducted, such as the duration of the experiment. It is possible that some drugs are more effective in the short term, but lose their effectiveness in the long term, and vice versa. The only suggestion we can confidently make here is that there appears to be reason for further research with a larger and more detailed dataset.
